BACKSWING

The backswing is one of the fundamental parts of a golf swing. It refers to the initial movement that a golfer makes in order to bring the club back before the downswing. The backswing sets the foundation for the rest of the swing and can greatly affect the outcome of the shot.

During the backswing, the golfer will start with their hands and clubhead low to the ground, and then begin to rotate their shoulders and hips to create momentum. As the golfer continues to rotate their body, the clubhead will begin to move upwards and backwards, with the hands and arms following along.

A key aspect of the backswing is maintaining a consistent spine angle. This means that the golfer should try to keep their upper body in the same position throughout the backswing, without leaning forward or backwards too much. This helps to ensure that the clubhead stays on the correct plane, which is important for accuracy and power.

DOWNSWING

The downswing is the second half of a golf swing, following the backswing. It is the motion in which a golfer brings the clubhead down towards the ball, with the goal of making solid contact and generating power and accuracy.

During the downswing, the golfer will begin by shifting their weight from their back foot to their front foot. This helps to generate momentum and power, which is then transferred to the clubhead as it swings down towards the ball. The golfer will also rotate their hips and shoulders towards the target, which helps to maintain a consistent swing plane and generate more speed.

One of the keys to a successful downswing is maintaining proper sequencing. This means that the golfer should start the downswing with their lower body, then their torso, and finally their arms and hands. This ensures that the clubhead is on the correct path and that the golfer is making solid contact with the ball.
